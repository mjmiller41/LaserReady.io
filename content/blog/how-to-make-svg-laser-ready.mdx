# How to Make an SVG Laser-Ready: The Complete File-Prep Checklist

**To make an SVG laser-ready, work through seven checks before you send it to the machine:
(1) close every cut path, (2) remove duplicate and overlapping lines, (3) set real-world units
and confirm the scale, (4) map colors to the right cut/engrave operations, (5) convert all text
to paths, (6) delete hidden raster images from cut layers, and (7) simplify paths that carry
thousands of nodes.** A file that passes all seven cuts on the first try. A file that fails even
one usually wastes a sheet of material to teach you which one.

Below is each check, why it matters on the bed, and exactly how to fix it in the vector editor
you already use.

> **Key takeaways**
> - "Looks right in the preview" and "cuts right on the laser" are two different things — a
>   preview renders pixels; a laser follows geometry.
> - The failure that burns the most material is the **open path**: a cut outline with a gap never
>   fully separates, so the part won't release.
> - Most fixes take seconds once you can *see* the problem — the hard part is that these defects

## What "laser-ready" actually means

A laser doesn't see your artwork. It sees **paths** (lines to follow) and **operations** (cut,
score, or engrave) mapped to those paths. "Laser-ready" means the file describes exactly the
geometry and operations you intend, in units the machine understands, with nothing extra that
will confuse the toolpath.

That is a stricter standard than "opens in Illustrator without errors." A file can look perfect
in every design tool and still fail on the bed, because design tools are built to render an image,
not to simulate a cut. The seven checks below are the gap between those two worlds.

## The 7-point laser-ready checklist

### 1. Close every cut path

**Why it matters:** A cut path has to be a closed loop for the part to fully separate from the
sheet. If an outline has even a tiny gap — two endpoints that look joined but aren't — the laser
traces right past the opening and the part stays attached by that sliver. Worse, an "open" path
that overlaps itself can double-burn one edge.

Open paths are the single most common reason a bought or AI-generated file won't cut cleanly, and
they are nearly impossible to spot by eye because the gap is often sub-millimeter.

**How to fix it:** Select the path and use your editor's *join* command (in Inkscape, select the
two end nodes and press `Shift+J` / **Join selected nodes**; in Illustrator, `Object > Path > Join`
or `Ctrl/Cmd+J`). For a whole file, closing paths one by one is tedious — this is exactly the kind
of defect a checker is worth running first, so you only hunt down the paths that are actually open.

### 2. Remove duplicate and overlapping lines

**Why it matters:** Two identical lines stacked on top of each other look like one line on screen,
but the laser cuts both — doubling the burn on that edge (charring, wider kerf) and doubling the
job time. Bundled marketplace files and files pasted together from multiple sources are the usual
source of duplicates.

**How to fix it:** In Inkscape/Illustrator, look for a *"remove duplicate"* or *"merge"* function,
or run a boolean **Union** on shapes that should be one outline. Watch for lines that are offset by
a hair rather than perfectly stacked — those need to be deleted manually.

### 3. Set real-world units and confirm the scale

**Why it matters:** SVG is a screen format; it does not guarantee a physical size. A file authored
in pixels can import as the wrong size, or as an ambiguous size the laser software has to guess at.
DXF has the same trap in reverse — the drawing units (mm vs inch vs unitless) may not match what
your laser software assumes. The result is a design that comes in at 10x or 1/25th the size you
expected, or simply doesn't fit the bed.

**How to fix it:** Set your document units explicitly (mm is safest for laser work) and set the
artboard/viewBox so 1 unit = 1 mm. After import, always confirm a known dimension — measure a part
you know should be, say, 50 mm and verify the software agrees before you cut.

### 4. Map colors to the right operations (cut vs engrave)

**Why it matters:** Most laser workflows decide *what to do* with a shape based on its color or
layer, not its shape. A filled shape that you meant to cut can come in as an engrave; a stroke in
the wrong color can be ignored entirely. This is the "RGB vs cut/engrave" mapping problem, and it's
why a design that renders as a nice black-and-white graphic can do the wrong operation everywhere.

**How to fix it:** Decide your convention up front and apply it consistently: e.g., pure red
(`#FF0000`) strokes = cut, black fills = engrave, blue = score. Make cut lines **strokes** (lines),
not fills, and reserve **fills** for engraves. Then confirm the mapping in your laser software's
layer/color panel before running.

### 5. Convert all text to paths

**Why it matters:** Text in an SVG references a **font**. If the machine or laser software doesn't
have that exact font installed, the text reflows to a substitute — or disappears. What cut on your
screen becomes garbled or missing on someone else's setup.

**How to fix it:** Select all text and convert it to outlines/paths (Inkscape: **Path > Object to
Path**; Illustrator: **Type > Create Outlines**, `Shift+Ctrl/Cmd+O`). Do this on a copy — once text
is a path you can no longer edit the words. After converting, re-check for open paths (step 1), as
outlined text occasionally introduces them.

### 6. Delete hidden rasters from cut layers

**Why it matters:** A laser can't *cut* a bitmap — it can only engrave one. A "cut file" that has a
PNG or JPG smuggled inside it (a common artifact of files traced or exported from image tools) will
either be ignored or engraved when you expected a cut, and it bloats the file. Because the raster
often sits under the vectors, you won't see it on screen.

**How to fix it:** Open the objects/layers panel and look for embedded image objects. Delete any
raster that isn't intentionally meant to be engraved. If you *do* want an engrave from an image,
put it on its own clearly labeled engrave layer.

### 7. Simplify paths with thousands of nodes

**Why it matters:** A smooth curve should be a handful of Bezier nodes. Auto-traced or
over-detailed files can carry a "curve" made of thousands of tiny segments. The laser head
stutters trying to follow every node, cut quality drops, and some software chokes on import.

**How to fix it:** Use your editor's **Simplify** command (Inkscape: **Path > Simplify**,
`Ctrl/Cmd+L`) in small doses, checking that the shape still looks right. For auto-traced art,
re-tracing at a lower detail setting often beats simplifying after the fact.

## A note on stroke width and "hairline" cuts

A frequent point of confusion: does the **stroke width** of a line control the cut? On most laser
software the machine follows the *centerline* of the path regardless of how thick the stroke looks
on screen — so a 5 px stroke and a 0.01 mm stroke cut the same line. What matters is that the cut
geometry is a **line (stroke)** and not a **filled shape**, and that it's mapped to the cut
operation (step 4). Some workflows do use a specific thin "hairline" stroke as the signal for
*"this is a cut, not an engrave,"* so match whatever convention your laser software expects — but
don't assume a thicker stroke cuts a wider slot. It doesn't.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is an SVG automatically laser-ready if it opens in my design software?**
No. Design software renders artwork; it doesn't verify cut geometry. A file can open and preview
perfectly and still have open paths, wrong units, or hidden rasters that only surface on the laser.

**Does stroke width control how wide the laser cuts?**
Generally no. Most laser software follows the path centerline, so stroke width is a visual property,
not a cut width. What matters is that cut lines are strokes (not fills) and mapped to the cut
operation. Some setups use a thin "hairline" stroke only as a *signal* that a line is a cut.

**Do I have to convert text to paths for laser cutting?**
Yes, in almost every case. Text references a font that the target machine may not have installed,
which causes the text to reflow or vanish. Converting text to outlines locks the shapes so they cut
as designed.

**What's the difference between preparing an SVG and a DXF for laser?**
The seven checks apply to both, but the common failure points differ: SVGs most often have unit/scale
ambiguity and hidden rasters, while DXFs most often have open polylines and unit mismatches (mm vs
inch). LaserReady checks both formats.

**Why won't my file cut even though it looks fine?**
Almost always one of the seven problems above — most often an open path or a color/layer mapped to
the wrong operation. See our companion guide,
